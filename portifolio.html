Problem-Solving & Innovation

Navigating Ambiguity in AI Development

Many projects, including FarmBuddy and NourishKids, were in early-stage development with evolving requirements and limited formal documentation. Rather than waiting for clarity, I took initiative to break down vague objectives into measurable milestones and developed lightweight prototypes to stimulate discussion and guide iteration.

Challenge: Incomplete datasets and API inconsistencies across platforms. For example, climate modeling had data gaps in temporal consistency and geographic granularity.
Solution: Collaborated with data science teams to clean, merge, and validate external datasets. Documented all assumptions and gaps. For NourishKids, integrated open-source language models and proposed batch processing to enhance utility with lightweight infrastructure.

Recommendation: Enhanced Cross-Team Collaboration

Working on NourishKids revealed that UI/UX and ML teams often worked on closely-related tasks without real-time alignment, causing delays and rework. I recommended implementing unified digital collaboration tools (Teams, Slack, or Notion) to:

  • Consolidate messaging, file storage, and version control
  • Enable real-time design-to-development feedback loops
  • Create centralized knowledge management systems
  • Improve transparency and accountability across departments

This systems-level thinking demonstrates my ability to identify not just technical challenges, but organizational patterns that limit impact, a key skill for the M.DevEng program.

Key Learnings

  • Accessibility first: The most powerful technology is the most accessible. Simple, well-designed interfaces often outperform sophisticated systems in resource-constrained contexts.
  • Data as responsibility: Working with vulnerable populations requires thoughtful data governance, informed consent, and protection against misuse.
  • Iteration over perfection: In dynamic environments, rapid prototyping and continuous feedback cycles are more valuable than lengthy planning phases.
  • Systems thinking: Individual technical solutions succeed only when embedded in supportive organizational structures and user communities.

Reflection & Future Vision

This internship experience fundamentally shaped how I think about technology as a tool for human agency and dignity. Working on FarmBuddy, NourishKids, and supporting systems exposed me to the full lifecycle of development technology, from ideation and participatory design, through technical implementation and user testing, to scaling and impact measurement.

What struck me most was how small design decisions have outsized impacts on user adoption. A woman farmer's decision to use FarmBuddy depends not on model accuracy alone, but on whether the interface respects her time, builds on her indigenous knowledge, and recognizes her expertise. Similarly, a parent's trust in NourishKids stems from understanding why a recommendation is made, transparency matters as much as performance.

These insights directly motivate my capstone research at UC Berkeley: How can we design AI systems that maintain calibrated uncertainty, communicate that uncertainty to users in accessible ways, and increase genuine adoption, not just technical uptake?

UC Berkeley's Master of Development Engineering is the ideal next step. It will equip me with rigorous evaluation frameworks, interdisciplinary knowledge spanning economics and implementation science, and access to faculty and networks dedicated to technology that serves marginalized communities. I leave this internship empowered, inspired, and ready to tackle larger challenges at the intersection of AI, development, and human dignity.
